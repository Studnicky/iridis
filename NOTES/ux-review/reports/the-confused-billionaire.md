# The Confused Billionaire — blind first look at "IRIDIS," some sort of color machine for people who own too many monitors

## Who I am
I buy companies before lunch and I have never once opened a "settings" menu — that is what people are for.

## My first five seconds
Black. A starfield. A little glowing jellyfish-eyeball thing floating next to a blob of melted crayons, and underneath it in big chrome video-game letters: **IRIDIS**. Handsome. Genuinely handsome — this is the kind of dark, expensive-looking screen I associate with a hedge fund's login page or a supercar's dashboard. My gut said "ooh, premium." Then my eyes drifted up and my gut recoiled, because the *entire top of the page* is a fifty-word wall of tiny gray tabs — UPLOAD, COMBINE, MANUAL, PALETTE, CVD VISION, SCHEMA & COMPLIANCE, DERIVATION RELATIONS, PANDA CSS, UNOCSS, RDF (TURTLE) — it looks like the spine of an encyclopedia I would never buy. So: five seconds, two emotions. "This is gorgeous" immediately mugged by "who did this to a menu?"

## What I think this is / who it's for
Best I can tell, you feed it a picture and it vomits out every possible color code a computer could ever want — I see hex numbers, I see sliders, I see the words "OKLCH-native" and `engine.run()` glowing at me like I'm supposed to nod. It is a color factory. For engineers. Specifically the kind of engineer who says "delta-E merger" at a dinner party and watches the table go quiet. It is emphatically **not** for me, and it knows it, and it doesn't care, which — grudgingly — I respect a little.

## Where my eyes went & what I'd tap first
The name pulled me first. Then, thank God, one honest thing: a fat glowing **UPLOAD** button with a little arrow. *Finally.* One verb. One thing to press. If every screen were as decisive as that button I'd have understood this in ten seconds. There's also a "Futuristic" dropdown and a Light/Dark toggle right up top, which is the kind of harmless knob I enjoy flicking. Everything else — the tabs, the tabs, the endless tabs — I would never touch, because touching one clearly summons *four more rows of tabs* (I saw it happen: click something and a whole second ribbon of PALETTE / CVD VISION / SCHEMA & COMPLIANCE unfurls). It's tabs all the way down.

## What confused, annoyed, or lost me
Where do I start.
- **Screen 2 (the sliders).** "Merge input cap · 128," "Histogram bits · 5," "Harmonize threshold · 10," and a paragraph explaining that something is "O(n²), so this bounds the work." I don't know what any of those words are doing next to each other and I felt personally insulted that they expected me to.
- **Screen 6 (the code dump).** A black box full of `--c-syntax-operator: #c1a0fb;` line after line after line. This is the screen where a normal person quietly closes the tab. It's the back office. Why is the back office on the front page?
- **The Pipeline list (screens 7–8).** "1. intake:hexHint," "2. derive:semanticHues," all the way to "12. derive:variant," half of them stamped **required** and some **disabled** in little pills. Twelve steps. Nobody sells me anything with a twelve-step *anything*.
- **The bottom (screens 9–10).** After ALL that, I scroll down and discover a second, entirely separate list — "1. What is Iridis," "6. Plugin Ecosystem," "9. Task Registry Reference," "10. Math Primitives Reference." A "What is this" section... at the *bottom*. If I have to scroll past a math reference to find out what the thing is, the thing has lost me. Put that FIRST. In one sentence. Bigger than the swatches.

## What I actually liked
I'll be fair, because there's obvious money and taste in here:
- **The look.** That deep space-black with the drifting dots and the soft purple glow is genuinely premium. Whoever chose this palette has a good eye — it feels like something with a waiting list.
- **The logo.** The iridescent eye-orb is a real logo, the kind you could put on a matte-black box and charge triple for.
- **The color swatch grids (screens 3, 5).** Rows and rows of tidy little colored tiles with names — even not knowing what they *do*, they're satisfying, like a drawer of expensive paint chips. Some had "AAA" and "AA" badges next to numbers, which whispered "certified, official, someone's being rigorous here," and rich people love the word certified.
- **That UPLOAD button and the Light/Dark toggle.** The two moments the whole site spoke plain English. More of that.

## On my phone vs the big screen
Honestly? The phone is *better*, and that never happens. On the big screen the tab-wall is a single suffocating slab across the top. On the phone those same tabs breathe — they stack into neat little pill-buttons with room around them, and the swatches and the code line up in one clean column instead of that busy three-column back-office look. The color cards on mobile (screen 6, the "syntax-string / Ratio 9.42 / AAA" tiles) look downright classy. Same content, but the phone made it feel curated instead of dumped. The one casualty is the code box — hex codes bleeding off the right edge on a tiny screen is even more pointless than on the desktop. But if you sat me down blind and made me pick which one costs more money, I'd pick the phone.

## My verdict
IRIDIS looks like a million dollars and explains itself like a tax form. There is real craftsmanship here — the space-black glamour, that gorgeous little orb, the obsessively neat color chips — buried under an avalanche of tabs, sliders, twelve-step pipelines, and raw code that answers questions I would never in my life ask. The tragedy is that the two clearest things on the whole page (one glowing UPLOAD button, one plain What-Is-This section) are the answer to its own problem, and it hid the second one at the very bottom under a *math reference*. Lead with the beauty, lead with one sentence, hide the machinery in a drawer, and I'd have believed this was a company worth acquiring. As it stands I believe it's a company worth acquiring *and then immediately handing to someone who can tell me, in under ten words, what it's for.*

**Score: 4 out of 10** — on my personal scale, where 10 is "I understood it instantly and felt richer for it" and 1 is "I closed the tab and fired someone." It earns every point on looks and loses the rest to the wall of words.
