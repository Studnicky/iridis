# The Mom — blind first look at "IRIDIS," some kind of color tool for people who make websites

**Who I am** — I'm a mom who runs the house on my phone: Facebook, my banking app, Etsy, the group text with the other soccer parents. I'm not a computer person, but I know when a screen is talking to me and when it's talking over my head.

## My first five seconds

Oh my. The very first thing at the top is a giant wall of little words — UPLOAD, COMBINE, MANUAL, PALETTE, CVD VISION, SCHEMA & COMPLIANCE, DERIVATION RELATIONS, and it just keeps GOING, four or five rows of them. It looks like the settings menu on the DVR that my husband set up and told me never to touch. My eyes didn't know where to land, so they kind of froze.

Then underneath there's a beautiful dark starry sky, a sparkly little jeweled ball like a Christmas ornament, and the word **IRIDIS** in a big chunky sci-fi font. It's honestly pretty — like the opening of a space movie. But pretty doesn't tell me what to DO. My gut said: "this is somebody's very fancy project, and I'm not sure I'm allowed in here."

## What I think this is / who it's for

Okay, my honest guess: it's a tool that takes a picture — a photo you upload — and pulls all the colors out of it to make a set of colors for something. Like when I pick colors for a party invitation on Etsy, but way, WAY more serious. There's a line that says it turns "any image into a full palette," and I saw grids of little color squares, so I think it's for picking colors.

But then there's a word — **OKLCH-native** — and a bit that literally says `engine.run()` with the parentheses and everything, like computer code sitting right there in the welcome message. So this is NOT for me. This is for the people who BUILD the apps I use. Web designers, programmers. The kind of person my nephew is. It's a professional's workshop, and I wandered in through the wrong door.

## Where my eyes went & what I'd tap first

After the frozen moment, my eyes finally found it: a big glowing purple **UPLOAD** button with a little arrow, right in the middle. Thank goodness. That's the one thing on the whole first screen that felt like "tap here," and I'd tap it. There's also a little dropdown that says "Futuristic" and a LIGHT / DARK toggle, which I understood — that's the same night-mode switch my phone has, so that felt friendly and familiar.

But that's about where my confidence ran out. Below the Upload button things get busy fast — sliders, a card that says SAMPLE, and that whole top wall of tabs is still hanging there the entire time, following me down the page like a menu I can't close.

## What confused, annoyed, or lost me

- **The wall of tabs (every single screen).** That block of thirty-something little labels never goes away. On the desktop it's four rows across the top; on my phone it's SEVEN rows and eats the entire screen before I've seen anything. It made me feel like I was already lost before I started.
- **The sliders and jargon (desktop screen 2, phone screen 2).** "Clustering algorithm," "Merge input cap," "Histogram bits," "Harmonize threshold," "Delta-E." There are paragraphs explaining these and I read one twice and still couldn't tell you what it does. It's like reading the ingredients on a medicine bottle.
- **The code screen (desktop 6, phone 7).** A whole box of `--c-background: #060102;` in pink and blue — real programming code, just sitting there. I know that's normal for a developer, but for me it was a wall that said "turn around."
- **The giant table of colored squares (desktop 5, phone 6)** — dozens of them, each with "Ratio 9.85 AAA" or "fail" under it. I have no idea what passes or fails or why, and the word "fail" in red made me a little nervous, like I'd done something wrong.
- **The lower part is friendlier but LONG.** Way down at the bottom there's finally a plain-English list — "1. What is Iridis," "2. The Four Stages," "7. CLI Usage" — the kind of help menu I understand. But I had to scroll past a mountain of the scary stuff to reach it. That explanation should be near the TOP, holding my hand, not buried at the very bottom.

## What I actually liked

I want to be fair, because somebody clearly put a lot of love into this. It is genuinely beautiful — the deep starry background with the little colored dots twinkling is soothing, and the colors are gorgeous, not garish. The glowing purple buttons are easy to spot and look tappable. I liked that the LIGHT/DARK switch was something I already knew. And the little numbered accordion list at the bottom (1, 2, 3… with the little tidy rows and the arrow to open each) is exactly the kind of thing I'd happily poke at — clean, clearly labeled, one topic per row. When the design slows down and speaks plainly, it's actually lovely and inviting.

## On my phone vs the big screen

On the big computer screen it's a lot, but at least the tabs sit in neat rows across the top and I can see the starry picture and the IRIDIS title and the Upload button all at once — the whole "welcome" fits in one eyeful. On my **phone** it's rougher: that tab wall becomes seven stacked rows and swallows almost the entire first screen, so I have to scroll a long way just to reach the pretty part and the Upload button. The color squares and code became little left-right sliding rows with tiny arrows on the sides, which is fiddly with a thumb — I kept feeling like I'd swipe the wrong thing. The plain-language accordion at the bottom, though, works nicely on the phone — big tappable bars, easy to read. So: the phone is fine for the SIMPLE parts and overwhelming for the complicated parts, and this site is mostly complicated parts.

## My verdict

This is a stunning, serious tool that was clearly built by clever people for other clever people — and it never once stopped to tell a regular person like me what it's for or reassure me I'm in the right place. It's like walking into a professional's workshop where every beautiful machine is humming and labeled with words I don't know. I never felt like it was going to steal my information — it felt safe, just far, FAR over my head. If it opened with one plain sentence ("Turn a photo into a set of website colors") and a single friendly button, and tucked all those tabs and sliders behind a "for advanced users" door, I'd have felt welcome instead of shooed away.

I score this on my "would I know what to do without calling my nephew?" scale: **4 out of 10.** Two of those points are just for how genuinely pretty it is.
